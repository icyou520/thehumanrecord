A person walks along a beach covered with thousands of stranded starfish, washed ashore by the tide. They pick one up and throw it back into the ocean. A passerby says, "There are thousands. You can't possibly make a difference."

The person picks up another starfish, throws it into the waves, and says: "It made a difference to that one."

Loren Eiseley's original essay, "The Star Thrower," is more complex and melancholy than the simplified version that became famous. But the simplified version endures because it answers the most paralyzing objection to moral action: "The problem is too big, so why bother?"

The parable does not claim that individual action solves systemic problems. It does not deny that scale matters. It makes a narrower, more durable point: the value of saving one life is not diminished by the existence of other lives you cannot save. The person in front of you is not a rounding error.

This is the daily courage that most ethical life actually requires — not grand gestures, but the decision to act within your reach even when the full problem is beyond it.
